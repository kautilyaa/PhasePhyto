\begin{abstract}
This project looks at how plant disease classifiers behave when they are trained on clean lab images and then tested on real field images. We use PlantVillage~\cite{Hughes2015} as the source dataset and PlantDoc~\cite{Singh2020} as the target. A plain Vision Transformer fine tuned on PlantVillage with no extra tricks reaches only 28.76\% accuracy on PlantDoc. Adding a layered training recipe (label smoothing, differential learning rates, exponential moving average, sharpness aware minimization, RandAugment, random erasing, an HSV foreground mask, horizontal flip test time augmentation, and TENT) lifts the same backbone to 52.29\%. That is a 23.5 point gain and is the main practical result of the project. We also built PhasePhyto, a three stream architecture that adds a fixed Phase Congruency feature stream and a CLAHE illumination stream on top of the ViT backbone, fused through cross attention. Even though Phase Congruency is mathematically invariant to amplitude changes, the full PhasePhyto model and the backbone only variant land at the same target accuracy (0.5229), and a Phase Congruency only variant collapses to 9.15\% on the target. So the recipe is the part that actually transfers. In a separate three class apple overlap benchmark across PlantVillage, PlantDoc, and Plant Pathology 2021 ($n{=}11{,}310$), softer class rebalancing during training raises macro F1 from 0.6813 to 0.7015, and beats post hoc prior calibration even with an oracle prior.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Plant disease classifiers tend to look very strong on benchmark splits but fall apart on field images. PlantVillage images have isolated leaves on uniform backgrounds with stable lighting. PlantDoc images have clutter, occlusion, weird angles, and harsh lighting. The gap between the two is wide enough to use it as an out of distribution (OOD) benchmark on its own. In our setup, a plain ViT-B/16 fine tuned on PlantVillage gets 28.76\% accuracy on PlantDoc, which is a useful starting point for measuring how much a training recipe can help.

Our starting hypothesis was that Phase Congruency (PC) features could close part of that gap. PC is a frequency domain operator that measures local phase alignment across a log-Gabor filter bank, and it is provably invariant to monotonic amplitude scaling~\cite{Kovesi1999}. The intuition was that PC should pick up lesion edges and structural texture without caring about the lighting. We built PhasePhyto on top of that idea: a three stream model that combines fixed PC feature maps, a pretrained Vision Transformer backbone (ViT-B/16)~\cite{Dosovitskiy2021}, and a CLAHE based illumination stream, with cross attention fusion between the PC and ViT streams.

Two questions ran through the project:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Does PC fusion actually help OOD transfer on plant disease images?
    \item Which training and test time choices give reliable gains regardless of architecture?
\end{enumerate}

The answer to the first is no on this benchmark. The answer to the second is yes, and the recipe we ended up with closes the plain baseline gap by 23.5 points and works across every model variant we tried. The rest of the paper goes through related work, methodology, results, and what we take away.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{Deep Learning for Plant Disease Detection}

Mohanty et al.~\cite{Mohanty2016} showed that convolutional neural networks (CNNs) trained on PlantVillage can hit over 99\% in distribution accuracy across 26 disease categories. They explicitly noted that this was on controlled lab style images and did not test field transfer. Hughes and Salathe~\cite{Hughes2015} reported similar lab numbers and flagged the gap to field conditions as the open problem. Singh et al.~\cite{Singh2020} introduced PlantDoc to make that gap measurable, and reported that ImageNet pretrained networks moved from PlantVillage to PlantDoc reach roughly 20 to 52\% accuracy depending on architecture and on how much PlantDoc specific preprocessing is used. Our plain ViT-B/16 baseline of 28.76\% lands inside that range, which is a sanity check that our setup is consistent with prior work.

\subsection{Domain Adaptation and OOD Robustness}

Test time adaptation has become a popular way to close domain gaps without target labels. TENT~\cite{Wang2021} updates only the affine parameters of normalization layers at inference time so that the predictions on target batches have lower entropy. Sharpness aware minimization (SAM)~\cite{Foret2021} looks for flat regions of the loss surface and tends to generalize better OOD. Exponential moving average (EMA) weight averaging~\cite{Izmailov2018} smooths the optimization trajectory and also helps generalization. Our recipe uses all three, on top of strong augmentation. The ablation in Section~\ref{sec:results} suggests these recipe pieces account for most of the OOD gain we see.

\subsection{Physics-Informed and Invariant Feature Extraction}

Phase congruency, introduced by Kovesi~\cite{Kovesi1999}, measures how consistent local phase is across spatial frequency bands. Unlike standard gradient based edge detectors, the output is invariant to monotonic amplitude transforms, which makes it appealing in tasks where lighting changes between train and test. PC has been used in medical imaging and texture classification, but to our knowledge there is no controlled study of PC for OOD transfer in plant pathology, so that is what we set out to provide.

Different papers use different label sets and test splits, so the comparison is meant as a sanity check rather than a head to head ranking.

The model has three parallel streams, shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:architecture}.

The \textbf{PC stream} converts the input RGB image to grayscale, runs a log-Gabor filter bank (4 scales, 8 orientations), and produces three structural maps: PC magnitude, phase symmetry, and oriented energy. A small CNN (about 150K parameters) turns those maps into structural tokens. The \textbf{semantic stream} feeds the RGB image into a ViT-B/16 backbone (about 86M parameters)~\cite{Dosovitskiy2021}, which gives 197 patch tokens of dimension 768. The \textbf{illumination stream} applies CLAHE normalization to flatten global lighting differences and then encodes the result through a shallow CNN (about 150K parameters) into a small auxiliary embedding.

In the full model, the structural tokens from the PC stream act as queries against the ViT patch tokens through 8 head cross attention with fusion dimension 256. The fused result is concatenated with the illumination embedding and passed to the classification head (about 138K parameters). The \textbf{backbone only} ablation drops the PC stream and the cross attention. The \textbf{no fusion} ablation keeps all three streams but skips cross attention and uses simple concatenation. The \textbf{PC only} ablation keeps just the PC and illumination streams. The full configuration totals around 87M parameters, almost all in the ViT-B/16 backbone.

The PC filter bank has no trainable parameters. The log-Gabor wavelets are defined analytically and held fixed. Only the downstream token encoder, the cross attention block, and the classifier head are trained. That makes the PC branch a clean test of whether physics inspired fixed features help OOD transfer when the classifier on top is still trained on the source domain.

\subsection{Training Recipe}
\label{sec:recipe}

Table~\ref{tab:config} lists the hyperparameters we used. The most important pieces are these:

\begin{table}[ht]
\caption{PhasePhyto training configuration.}
\label{tab:config}
\centering
\begin{tabular}{ll}
\toprule
Hyperparameter & Value \\
\midrule
Backbone & ViT-B/16 (ImageNet-21k pretrained) \\

HSV saturation threshold & 40 \\

TENT steps / LR & 20 / $10^{-3}$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\textbf{Differential learning rates.} The pretrained ViT runs at 10 times lower learning rate than the rest. This keeps the backbone close to its pretrained weights while letting the fusion head and PC encoder adapt.

\textbf{Label smoothing.} With $\varepsilon = 0.1$~\cite{Szegedy2016}, the model is less likely to be over confident on PlantVillage and so calibration on PlantDoc is better.

\textbf{EMA.} A shadow copy of the weights is updated at decay 0.999 every step~\cite{Izmailov2018}. We evaluate and checkpoint with the EMA weights.

\textbf{SAM.} The two pass update minimizes the loss in a small neighborhood of the current parameters~\cite{Foret2021}. Flat optima generalize better to new domains.

\textbf{HSV foreground gating.} Saturation thresholding in the HSV color space picks out the leaf, and background pixels get replaced with random crops. This makes it harder for the model to take a shortcut through background texture.

\textbf{Test time pieces.} At evaluation, we average predictions on the image and its horizontal flip (TTA), and we run TENT~\cite{Wang2021} for 20 steps to adapt the normalization affines to the target distribution.

\subsection{Datasets and Evaluation Protocol}

\textbf{PlantVillage}~\cite{Hughes2015} is the source domain (87,848 training images, 25 mapped disease classes). \textbf{PlantDoc}~\cite{Singh2020} is the primary OOD target (153 mapped test samples in the same 25 classes). The target set is small, which is a real limitation. We address it partly with a second benchmark.

For the \textbf{Apple Overlap Benchmark}, we take the three classes that appear in PlantVillage, PlantDoc, and Plant Pathology 2021~\cite{Thapa2020}: \textit{Apple healthy}, \textit{Apple scab}, and \textit{Cedar apple rust}. PP2021 gives a much larger target split with $n{=}11{,}310$ samples, which lets us tell whether intervention effects are real or just noise. We report accuracy and macro F1 (the unweighted mean of per class F1) throughout.

\subsection{Main 25 Class OOD Ablation}

Table~\ref{tab:ablation} has the central ablation. The full model and the backbone only variant land at the same target accuracy of 0.5229 and differ by only 0.009 macro F1, so the PC stream is not adding measurable OOD signal under a shared recipe. Both beat the plain ViT baseline by 23.5 points, which is the main thing we wanted to know: the recipe is what is doing the work, not the architecture. The PC only variant collapses to 9.15\% target accuracy even though it reaches 73.40\% on the source, which says structural feature invariance alone is not enough for OOD classification.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\textbf{Invariance does not propagate to the classifier head.} The most important takeaway is that amplitude invariant features are not automatically classifier invariant. The PC operator does what it claims at the feature level, but the classifier head can still overfit to source specific co occurrences that happen to be present in the invariant features. The PC only run reaches 73.40\% source accuracy (so the structural features carry useful signal) and then drops to 9.15\% on the target, a 64 point gap. The full and backbone only models drop by about 47.5 points each. So the PC stream actually produces a larger absolute target drop than the ViT backbone alone, even though the features themselves are formally invariant. Invariance at the input does not stop the head from memorizing source statistics during training.

\textbf{Cross attention is a stabilizer, not an amplifier.} Comparing the full and no fusion variants tells us that removing cross attention while keeping the PC branch hurts target performance (49.02\% vs 52.29\%), but removing the PC branch entirely (backbone only) lands at the same number as the full model. So cross attention is mostly absorbing damage that the PC branch introduces, rather than producing new OOD signal. In any setting where you cannot remove a physics inspired branch, cross attention gating is a safer fusion choice than concatenation.

\textbf{The recipe is the contribution.} Going from a plain ViT baseline at 28.76\% to a recipe hardened model at 52.29\% is the result we are most confident in. Each piece is individually motivated. SAM and EMA push the optimizer toward flat regions. The HSV mask removes background shortcuts. TENT adapts normalization to target batches at inference time. The fact that the gain holds across all three recipe containing variants (full, backbone only, no fusion) is what convinces us the recipe is the cause and not any specific architecture choice.

\textbf{Calibration vs feature shift.} The apple overlap results are unambiguous about where the residual gap on PP2021 lives. Even an oracle prior calibration cannot beat the uncalibrated baseline on macro F1. So most of what is left is genuine feature shift, especially for cedar apple rust, and it has to be addressed during training (Fix B rebalancing) rather than after the fact (Fix A logit adjustment).

\textbf{Limitations.} The 25 class PlantDoc target split has only 153 mapped samples, so any difference of two points or smaller should not be over interpreted. The apple overlap study is a single seed sweep. Multi seed runs and a transductive variant of TENT applied to the full PP2021 target are the natural next steps. They would tell us whether the rust gap is mostly adaptation limited or architecture limited.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

The most useful thing this project produced is a reproducible OOD recipe for plant disease classification. On PlantVillage to PlantDoc, the recipe lifts a plain ViT-B/16 from 28.76\% to 52.29\% target accuracy without changing the architecture in any clever way. The recipe is architecture agnostic, simple to apply, and works across every variant we tested.

The architectural side of the project is more cautious. Phase Congruency features are mathematically invariant to amplitude scaling and the PhasePhyto model does pay attention to lesion structure when we look at the cross attention maps. But the full model and the backbone only variant land at the same target accuracy on this benchmark, which means the PC branch did not turn formal feature invariance into measurable OOD gains. We treat that as a clean signal that physics inspired feature streams need empirical validation before they are claimed as a robustness mechanism.

The apple overlap benchmark adds one more practical lesson. When source and target priors differ, training time class rebalancing beats post hoc prior calibration, and that is true even with an oracle prior. So if you are deploying a model across distributions with shifted class frequencies, fixing the gap during training is more reliable than trying to repair it at inference.
