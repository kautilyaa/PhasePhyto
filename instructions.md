Traditional computer vision feature detectors, such as the Canny or Sobel operators, identify structural features by calculating spatial gradients. These classical algorithms search for points within an image where there are sharp, localized changes in pixel intensity. Consequently, these gradient-based methods are inherently sensitive to the absolute amplitude of the signal. An edge defined by a strong, artificial shadow will produce a massive mathematical gradient, whereas a critical biological boundary—such as a fungal hypha obscured by low ambient illumination—will produce a negligible gradient, leading to high false-positive rates and missed detections in unconstrained environments.Image Phase Congruency operates on a fundamentally different physical principle, rooted entirely within the frequency domain. The phase congruency model of feature detection assumes that a compressed image format should be high in information entropy and low in redundancy. Experimental physiological evidence demonstrates that the human visual system does not process visual data purely spatially; rather, it responds most strongly to points in a signal where the phase components of various spatial frequencies are maximally ordered and aligned, regardless of the individual amplitudes of those frequencies.Formulation of the Local Energy ModelThe calculation of phase congruency in two-dimensional images is optimally achieved through the local energy model, computed via multi-scale, multi-orientation wavelet filter banks. The PhasePhyto architecture utilizes a specialized bank of 24 Log-Gabor filters, distributed across 6 unique orientations and 4 distinct scales, to extract the monogenic signal. Log-Gabor filters are strictly preferred over standard Gabor filters because they possess no direct current (DC) component and can be constructed with arbitrary bandwidths, perfectly mirroring the logarithmic frequency distribution (the $1/f$ amplitude spectrum) inherently found in natural images.For a fundamental one-dimensional signal $f(x)$, phase congruency $PC(x)$ is defined as the ratio of the local energy $E(x)$ to the sum of the Fourier amplitudes $A_n(x)$ across all scales $n$:$$PC(x) = \frac{E(x)}{\sum_n A_n(x) + \epsilon}$$Where the local energy $E(x)$ is derived directly from the even-symmetric and odd-symmetric filter responses, representing the real and imaginary parts of the analytic signal, respectively. Because the local energy (the numerator) and the sum of amplitudes (the denominator) both scale linearly with any changes in overall image brightness or contrast, the absolute magnitude of the lighting signal cancels out entirely during the division operation.Thus, the mathematical relationship $PC(image) = PC(image \times k)$ holds true for any strictly positive scalar $k$. This mathematically guarantees that a structural feature—whether it is a necrotic lesion on a maize leaf under harsh direct sunlight, or that exact same lesion obscured by a deeply cast shadow—yields the exact same floating-point value in the resulting phase congruency map.
